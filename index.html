Introduction

Indomethacin is a non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) used to treat mild to moderate acute pain and relieve symptoms of arthritis (osteoarthritis and rheumatoid arthritis) or gout, such as inflammation, swelling, stiffness, and joint pain.

Chemical Information

Chemical Formula

C19H16ClNO4

Therapeutic Category
  • Anti-Inflammatory Agents
  • Non-Steroidal

Structural formula of Indomethacin. Source: PubChem

Dosage Forms & Strengths (Adults)

  • Capsule: 25 mg, 50 mg
  • Capsule (Extended Release): 75 mg
  • Oral Suspension: 25 mg/5 ml
  • Suppository: 50 mg

Mechanism of Action

Indomethacin works by blocking an enzyme called cyclo-oxygenase (COX), which is involved in making substances known as prostaglandins. It is believed to help close a patent ductus arteriosus (PDA) by reducing the production of prostaglandins, particularly PGE1, encouraging the ductus arteriosus to close in premature babies.

Indications

  • Management of acute gouty arthritis
  • Used in combination to treat joint pain
  • Management of moderate to severe osteoarthritis
  • Management of moderate to severe rheumatoid arthritis

Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Indomethacin is rapidly absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract.

Bioavailability

100% (Oral)

Onset

30 min

Duration

4-6 hr

Peak Plasma Time

0.5-2 hr; 1.67 hr

Volume of Distribution

Ranges from 0.34 to 1.57 L/kg via oral, rectal, or intravenous administration. Indomethacin crosses the blood-brain barrier (BBB), with only the unbound fraction diffusing across due to extensive plasma protein binding.

Protein Binding

Indomethacin is a weak organic acid, 90-99% bound to plasma protein (albumin), but does not bind to red blood cells.

Metabolism

Metabolized in the liver, undergoes enterohepatic circulation where it is excreted into bile as glucuronide, then reabsorbed after breakdown in the intestine.

Route of Elimination

  • Renal excretion
  • Biliary excretion
  • Urine (60%)
  • Feces (>33%)

Half-Life

Biphasic elimination:

  • First phase: ~1 hour
  • Second phase: 2.6 to 11.2 hours
  • Oral average: ~4.5 hours
  • Intravenous average: ~20 hours

Clearance

1 to 2.5 mL/kg/min when taken orally.

Administration

Orally

Take with food or 8-12 oz of water to avoid gastrointestinal effects.

Intravenous

Infuse over 20-30 min at a concentration of 0.5-1 mg/mL in preservative-free sterile water for injection (SWI) or normal saline (NS). Avoid bolus administration to prevent vasoconstriction and compromised intestinal blood flow. Do not administer intra-arterially.

Contraindications

Absolute

History of severe allergy to indomethacin or aspirin (e.g., rash, asthma, anaphylaxis).

Relative (Caution Needed)

  • Bleeding problems
  • Peptic and duodenal ulcers
  • Mouth inflammation (stomatitis)
  • Neonates: premature kidney issues, ongoing infection, necrotizing enterocolitis, active bleeding (e.g., stomach or brain)

Pregnancy & Lactation

Pregnancy

Avoid after 30 weeks due to risk of early fetal ductus arteriosus closure and kidney problems. If needed between 20-30 weeks, use the lowest effective dose for the shortest time. Monitor for oligohydramnios if used >48 hours; stop if it occurs.

Pregnancy Category
  • First Trimester: Category B (generally safe)
  • Third Trimester: Category D (evidence of risk, avoid unless necessary)

Lactation

Indomethacin may pass into breast milk. Weigh benefits of breastfeeding and mother's need against risks to the baby.

Interactions

  1. Captopril (ACE Inhibitor): Reduces blood pressure-lowering effect, may harm kidney function.
    Caution: Avoid combination or consider alternatives.
  2. Methotrexate: Increases methotrexate levels by slowing renal clearance, risking blood and gut toxicity.
    Caution: Avoid due to severe toxicity risk.
  3. Aminolevulinic Acid (Oral): Increases phototoxic side effects, especially around surgery.
    Caution: Avoid phototoxic drugs within 24 hours of aminolevulinic acid.

Off-Label Use

  • Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA)
  • Juvenile idiopathic arthritis
  • Reduce calcium levels in the blood
